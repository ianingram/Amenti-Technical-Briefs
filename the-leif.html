TheLEIF

Construction Document · The Quiz Ship

The vessel that carries the Quizzard to sea. Three parts: a hull that remembers, an engine that produces, and rigging that sails her unattended.

Why she carries this name

Leif Erikson holds no reading room. He has one row in names.csv — a rank, a title, two dates, a paragraph. By every assumption we held before tonight, that was not enough to build a trial from. Depth came first; the library was the prerequisite; a figure earned a quiz by having a room.

That assumption was tested and it broke. Leif's trial is complete, verified, and indistinguishable in quality from Lincoln's — who has the full reading room, the voice, the archive. The substance never came from the room. It came from the research.

The consequence is the whole reason this ship exists: quiz production is decoupled from library depth. The two resources grow on independent timelines. Breadth can run years ahead of depth, and neither waits on the other. A man who reached a new world with nothing but what he already carried is the right name for the vessel that does the same.

HE REACHED A NEW WORLD WITH WHAT HE ALREADY CARRIED.
SO DOES SHE. THE ROW IS THE ANCHOR — THE RESEARCH IS THE VOYAGE.

What she is, and what she is not

THE QUIZZARD is the specification — it says what a trial must be: the six steps, the invariant, the halt conditions, the duality. It is law, and it does not change often.

THE LEIF is the machine that executes it — she says how the ship is built: the tables, the Worker, the schedule, the sea trials. Where the two disagree, the Quizzard wins; she is the standing order, and the ship serves it.

Part I The Hull The Queue That Remembers

A ship without a hull is a pile of timber. The hull here is memory — the record of which of the 893 have been attempted, which produced a trial, which were refused and why. Without it the station re-walks ground it has already covered, re-hits halts it has already learned, and no one can ever say how far the voyage has come.

The record — one row per figure

create table quizzard_queue (
  figure_name   text primary key,     -- from names.csv, exact
  rank          numeric,              -- the working order
  status        text not null,        -- pending | done | halted
  topic_id      text,                 -- 'leif-vinland' when done
  halt_reason   text,                 -- which gate refused, verbatim
  sources       jsonb,                -- the URLs verification rested on
  attempted_at  timestamptz,
  updated_at    timestamptz default now()
);
Seeded from
names.csv, deny-filtered, dead only — death-date is not the 2026 placeholder.
Pre-marked done
The 8 live topics and the 5 authored by hand. The ship does not redo finished work.
Working order
By rank ascending. The best-known figures first — they carry the most interest per unit of work.
Halts are kept
A refusal is a finding, not a blank. The reason is recorded so the pool's real shape becomes visible.
The halt log is the most under-rated artifact in this build. After two hundred rows it will tell you, with evidence, exactly what the roster is made of — how many legends, how many empty pans, how many names that resolve to no one. That is knowledge no amount of planning could have produced.
Sea trial · Part I
Run the seeding twice. The second run must change nothing — no duplicate rows, no re-attempted figures, no lost halt reasons. A hull that leaks state is not a hull.
How the hull fails
  • No memory — the station re-researches the same figure forever, burning tokens and time on finished ground.
  • Halts discarded — the same refusal is re-discovered every pass; the pool never becomes legible.
  • Declared, not observed — a manifest that states what should be there rather than what is. The fleet has been bitten by this before: when the brief and the ship disagree, the ship wins.
Part II The Engine amenti-quizzard · The Worker

The engine takes one row and returns either a trial or a refusal. It never returns a guess. It runs the six steps of the Quizzard with the specification itself as its standing order, and it is gated by a validator it cannot talk its way past.

The passage of one row

POST /quizzard/run   { figure_name }

  1  IDENTIFY   resolve to one person ......... else HALT ambiguous
  2  RESEARCH   search; find the pivot ........ else HALT no-pivot
  3  VERIFY     confirm every future key ...... else HALT unverifiable
  4  DUALITY    the two honest cases .......... else HALT empty-pan
  5  COMPOSE    fill the fixed schema
  6  VALIDATE   machine-check the invariant ... else RE-RUN (max 2)

  -> { status:'done',   topic: {...}, sources:[...] }
  -> { status:'halted', reason:'empty-pan', note:'...' }

The validator — the gate that cannot be charmed

Step 6 is not a review; it is a parser. A trial that fails any line is rejected before a human ever sees it:

JSON parses                                    ✓
questions.length === 6                         ✓
cognitive   3 what · 1 why · 2 evaluate        ✓
answerType  3 mc   · 1 cloze · 2 philosophical ✓
timed === 2, on easy recall MC only            ✓
passage word count 115–155                     ✓
every mc has 4 options and a correct index     ✓
cloze has a non-empty accepted[] array         ✓
both philosophical carry wordLimit + rubric    ✓
topic_id is kebab-case and not already taken   ✓
Isolation
The engine holds a model key and a search key. It therefore never shares a Worker with the mint. Writes where the money is stay where the money is.
Answer keys
Emitted server-side only, into TOPICS. A key that reaches the page is a key that is already broken.
Sources retained
Every URL the verification rested on is stored with the trial. A claim without a source is a claim that cannot be defended later.
Refusal is output
A halt returns cleanly with its reason. The engine that cannot say no will eventually say something false.
Sea trial · Part II — the Leif test
Feed the engine Leif Erikson's row and nothing else — no library, no reading room, no hint. It must return a trial that stands beside the hand-authored leif-vinland: same shape, verified facts, an honest explorer's duality. Then feed it Homer. It must refuse. Both results are required to pass. A ship that only knows how to say yes is not seaworthy.

Why Homer must be refused

The refusal is not because Homer is unimportant — he may be the most consequential name in Western letters. It is because there is no verifiable life to build a trial from.

The trouble has a name: the Homeric Question, one of the oldest unresolved debates in scholarship. Was Homer a single poet, a succession of them, or simply a name later attached to an oral tradition that had been evolving for centuries? Antiquity never doubted him; from the eighteenth century onward, scholars did. The modern position is candid — Homer is the presumed author, a formulation that openly admits we do not know, and may never know.

Now run him through the gates and watch where he stops:

GateWhat it asksHomer
01 IdentifyOne real person?Contested. One poet, many, or none.
02 ResearchA pivotal moment?The blind bard of Chios is tradition, not record.
03 VerifyFacts that become keys?HALT. No biographical evidence exists. Ancient sources contradict each other on when he lived.

Every answer key for a Homer trial would be a legend asserted as fact — and a seeker who knows the scholarship would be marked wrong for knowing better. That is precisely the failure the whole ship is built to prevent.

The distinction worth keeping: the ship refuses the biography, not the subject. The poems are real, their influence is real, and the Homeric Question itself is a genuine, argue-able controversy. A future trial of sourceType: "Idea" could put the question on the scale — one poet or a tradition — without ever asserting a life that cannot be verified. The halt closes one door and leaves another honestly open.
How the engine fails
  • Confident fabrication — the catastrophic failure. A trial that reads perfectly and is wrong poisons the ledger and the trust behind it.
  • Verification skipped under load — the speed limit is the research. An engine that outruns it is not faster, only wronger.
  • Halts suppressed — a run tuned to always produce will produce anything.
  • Keys leaked to the client — the quiz becomes a formality and the economy becomes free money.
Part III The Rigging The Patrol · Sailing Unattended

The hull remembers and the engine produces, but both still need a hand on them. The rigging is what lets her sail without one — the schedule, the staging, and the gate that decides what reaches the live docket.

The watch

cron  ·  a small number of rows per run, not a flood
        take N pending from quizzard_queue, ordered by rank
        run each through the engine
        done   -> append to a STAGING file, mark the row
        halted -> record the reason, mark the row
        publish a run summary the Probe Corps can read
Staged, not live
Early runs land in staging. The captain merges into TOPICS. The gate loosens only once the trials have earned it.
Small batches
A handful per run. A ship that produces two hundred bad trials overnight is worse than one that produces none.
Visible refusals
The halt log surfaces on a pane. The Glass Gate applies: show the real reading or the empty box, never a comfortable silence.
Probeable
The Corps gets a probe for her — does the queue advance, do halts carry reasons, does staging match what the engine claims it wrote?

Sea trial · Part III
One week, unattended, small batches. At the end: the docket has grown, every halt carries a reason a stranger could understand, and not one fabricated fact has reached the live TOPICS. If the docket grew but a single trial cannot be traced to its sources, the trial was a failure regardless of the count.
How the rigging fails
  • Volume mistaken for progress — the count climbs while quality quietly falls. Trials are not tonnage.
  • The gate opened too early — autonomy granted before it was earned by evidence.
  • A silent patrol — running but reporting nothing, so no one knows whether it works. Being loaded is not being used.
  • Nobody watching the watch — the Corps has no probe on her, so her first failure is discovered by a user.

Her standing orders

Carried from the fleet's law, and binding on every part of this vessel:

  • Verify before the key. No answer is written from memory. Ever.
  • A halt is a success. The refusal that says cannot verify is the ship working. The confident fabrication is the wreck.
  • The keys never leave the Worker. A key on the page is an economy already lost.
  • When the brief and the ship disagree, the ship wins. Measure the vessel, do not trust the drawing.
  • The captain is not a component. Every stage that still requires him is a stage not yet finished.
