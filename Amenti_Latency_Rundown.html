Latency Rundown

Where the time actually goes in a chat turn — measured, traced, and ranked. What is already fast, what is not, and what to fix.

What we measured

A probe fired ten requests at the live voice Worker on the sixteenth of July, 2026, reading the cache label from each. The numbers are real, from one run and one location:

MISS
a fresh utterance, generated by the TTS: median 6,103 ms to full audio (min 4,348 / max 12,733).
HIT
a repeated utterance, served from the R2 cache: median 1,936 ms (min 865 / max 3,705).
3.2×
the measured cache speedup. Every hit returned the identical 96,570-byte file — a true cache hit, proven.

These times are whole-reply times. What a listener actually feels is different, and better — which is the first important finding.

What we traced in the code

Reading the actual speak path settled two questions the probe could not. One answer is good news; one is the opportunity.

STREAMS ✓
Voice playback already streams. The throttle fetches up to three measures at once (CHUNK_LOOKAHEAD = 2) and schedules them gaplessly — playback begins on measure one while later measures are still rendering. The listener hears the first sound as soon as the first measure is ready, not after the whole reply. The expensive, hard-to-build part is done and working.
BATCHES ✗
The text-to-voice handoff does not stream. Chat calls window.claude.complete().then(…) — which waits for the entire LLM reply to finish — and only then hands the whole finished text to the voice engine. The text half and the voice half run back to back, not overlapped.
FLOOR
The miss render is the hard floor. A first-time utterance is bound by the TTS render time itself, which the cache mitigates but cannot erase.

What the Speech Doctrine already knew

Before engineering anything, we checked this diagnosis against the fleet’s own Speech Doctrine — the record of the voice-engine consolidation. It corroborates the probe and adds three cautions that change how the fixes should be approached.

AGREES
A second probe already confirmed the cache. The doctrine’s probe7 found the R2 keys byte-identical and the archive a full hit — “nothing re-renders.” That is independent agreement with our latency probe’s identical-byte hits and 3.2× speedup. Two probes, one conclusion: the cache is solid and is not the problem. Do not touch it.
AGREES
The engine is already one. Four TTS engines — library.js, amenti-throttle.js, Page2.html, and a stowaway voice — were consolidated into one. There is no hidden second speak path adding time. The throttle we traced is the single engine.

The three cautions it adds

One — the mechanism is meant to resist you. The doctrine’s standing law: “the mechanism should be hard to change; the judgment should be trivial to change — because the mechanism is engineering (cache keys, state machines, streaming, chunk boundaries) and getting it wrong costs money and breaks trust.” Our two biggest fixes are mechanism changes, and streaming and chunk boundaries are named by name. This does not forbid the work — it weights it. The chunking convention that keeps the shared cache coherent must not be chased for speed.

Two — the handoff is entangled with the conversation state machine. The doctrine reveals the turn is gated by an _expecting flag, a microphone gate, and timeouts — a whole state machine, not just a speak call. So streaming the LLM into the voice engine (Fix 1) touches more than the handoff: it intersects the mic gate and the turn logic. It is a real refactor of the conversation turn, more entangled than a first glance suggests.

Three — being loaded is not being used. The doctrine’s hardest-won law: an entire rewrite was written, tested by three hundred assertions, and was not running — nothing had loaded it. Whatever latency fix is built, the last step is not a passing test; it is a live probe proving the change actually executes on the deployed path, and that the number moved. Build it, then measure it live.

The two documents agree where it counts: the cache is proven, the engine is single, and the mechanism is load-bearing. The Speech Doctrine does not soften the plan — it sharpens it, and it names the trap: a fix that exists but does not run.

The bottleneck, drawn

Because voice already streams, the felt latency is not the whole-reply number. It is the time for the LLM to finish, plus the time to render the first measure — and today those happen one after the other. If the LLM streamed its text into the voice engine sentence by sentence, the two would overlap, and first sound would arrive far sooner.

Figure — The HandoffToday the LLM finishes the whole reply before any voice begins. If it streamed sentence by sentence, voice would start on sentence one while the rest is still being written.

The fixes, ranked

Ranked by raw latency payoff below — but heed the Speech Doctrine’s caution: Fix 1 is the biggest win and the most entangled with the conversation state machine and the load-bearing mechanism. Fix 2 is the safer first move — it adds a new voice namespace without touching the streaming, the chunking, or the turn logic, so it can ship on its own while Fix 1 is designed carefully. Payoff and safety point at different first steps; that tension is the honest state of it.

FIX 1 · STREAM THE LLM INTO THE VOICE ENGINE — biggest win

Switch chat from complete-then-speak to speak-as-you-go: feed each sentence to the throttle the moment the LLM finishes it, rather than waiting for the whole reply. Time-to-first-sound collapses from (whole reply time + first render) toward (first sentence time + first render). The voice engine is already built to receive a stream; this is about feeding it one.

The dependency: this only works if window.claude.complete offers a streaming variant — a token or delta callback, or an async stream. If it is batch-only, a streaming completion path is needed first. This is the one open question to resolve before committing to the refactor.

The catch: it is a real refactor, not a one-line change. The completion handler also parses moves, accumulates the watchlist, and runs the turn-read reflection — and some of that can only run on the complete text. Streaming means restructuring so per-sentence voicing runs alongside whole-reply logic, carefully. And per the Speech Doctrine, it also intersects the _expecting flag, the mic gate, and the turn timeouts — so it is a refactor of the conversation turn, not just the speak call.

FIX 2 · A FASTER TTS TIER FOR LIVE CHAT — simpler, independent

The miss floor is the render time. Reserve the high-quality voice for the broadcast surfaces, where latency does not matter, and use a faster model for live chat's first response. This attacks the floor directly and does not depend on the streaming question above. It can ship on its own.

The caution: the measure-chunking convention (CHUNK_MAX, the style string) is locked — it is what keeps the shared cache coherent across surfaces. A faster model is a new voice namespace, which is fine; changing the chunking to chase speed would orphan the cache and must not be done casually.

FIX 3 · PRE-WARM COMMON OPENING MEASURES — exploits what exists

Replies often begin similarly. Pre-render a bank of common opening measures so the first measure of a reply is almost always a cache hit — served in hit-time (well under a second) while the novel measures render behind it. This exploits the streaming already in place and directly shortens time-to-first-sound. Lowest effort of the three.

The honest summary

You are in better shape than the six-second number suggested. The hard part — streaming, gapless voice playback — is already built and working. The remaining latency lives in two places: a batched text-to-voice handoff (Fix 1, the biggest win, gated on a streaming completion API), and the raw TTS render floor (Fix 2, independent and shippable now). Pre-warming (Fix 3) is a cheap accelerant on top.

And whichever is built, prove it live. The Speech Doctrine’s hardest law — being loaded is not being used — means the final step is not a green test but a re-run of the latency probe against the deployed path, confirming the number actually moved. Build, deploy, then measure on the glass.

VOICE ALREADY STREAMS. THE TEXT HANDOFF DOES NOT.
OVERLAP THE TWO, AND FIRST SOUND ARRIVES EARLY.
Every line of this note is traced from the deployed code or measured by the probe. The one thing not yet known — whether the completion API can stream — is named as the open question, not assumed. That is the Glass Gate rule applied to our own plan: state what is known, mark what is not.
