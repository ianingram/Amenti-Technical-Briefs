The Exchange

A strong in-game economy of earned tokens and collectible relics — engaging, scarce, tradable, and deliberately kept clear of real money. Build the living market first; value can come later, from strength, not shortcut.

THE ONE LAW ABOVE ALL OTHERS:
EMERALD TOKENS ARE EARNED, NEVER BOUGHT. THEY ARE NEVER CASHED OUT.
THE MOMENT ET TOUCHES REAL MONEY, THIS IS NO LONGER A GAME — IT IS A REGULATED EXCHANGE.

Everything below is designed to make a real, living economy — supply and demand, scarcity, prices that move, a marquee that finally means something — while ET stays a pure engagement currency with no cash value. This is the Pokémon-cards model, not the stock exchange. It is fun and defensible precisely because of the law above.

1 · The purpose test

Every mechanic answers one question: does it reward genuine engagement with history? The economy exists to make reading, quizzing, and arguing more rewarding — not to bolt gambling onto a library. When a mechanic drifts from that purpose (loot-box dopamine, pay-to-win, grind-for-grind), it is wrong, however "engaging." The relics are trophies of real learning.

2 · Faucets — where ET and relics come from

A faucet is any source that creates new ET or relics. Too generous and the currency inflates to worthlessness; too stingy and no one plays. The existing faucets (already live in the mint worker):

  • Quiz pass — earn ET for passing a legend's quiz. The core faucet.
  • Mastery bonus — extra ET for a perfect/mastery score.
  • Read-aloud — ET for listening, on a decaying curve (prevents idle farming).

New relic faucet (the collectible layer):

  • Relics are earned through mastery, not bought raw. Master Musashi's story → a chance at "Musashi's Blade." This ties scarcity to genuine achievement and keeps the faucet honest.
  • Avoid pure random loot-boxes: some jurisdictions regulate them even without cash value, and they drift from the purpose test. Prefer achievement-gated drops with a visible path ("3 more masteries to earn this relic").

3 · Sinks — where ET goes (the part most economies forget)

The live candlestick chart already shows the danger: ET only goes UP. Every faucet, no sink. Without sinks, ET inflates forever and the whole economy becomes meaningless — 10,000 ET buys what 100 ET used to. Sinks are not optional; they are what give ET value.

A sink is anything that destroys ET (burns it) or locks it up. Planned sinks:

  • Buying relics from the source shop — spend ET, it burns. The primary sink.
  • Trade fee — a small ET fee on every player-to-player trade, burned. Scales sink with market activity.
  • Endorsements / court votes — spend ET to back an argument (already a spend mechanic).
  • Relic upgrades / framing / display — optional ET sinks for prestige (cosmetic, no power).
  • The weekly pool — ET staked into the settling pool is temporarily removed from circulation.
A healthy economy is a balanced faucet and sink. The Exchange's job is to be the great sink that gives every earned token a reason to be spent. Watch the mint/burn ratio on the terminal — that is the economy's pulse.

4 · Scarcity — what makes a relic worth anything

If everyone can have Moses' sandals, they are worthless. Scarcity is the engine of value. Options, roughly increasing in strength:

ModelHow it worksFeel
Rarity tiersCommon / Rare / Legendary drop ratesFamiliar, clear
Limited editionsOnly N of each relic ever existUrgency, prestige
Numbered"Musashi's Blade #7 of 100"Collector identity
Era-lockedA relic only mintable during a season/eventFOMO, return visits

Recommendation: rarity tiers + numbered editions for legendary relics. Scarcity is what makes the marquee prices real — "Musashi's Blade ▲ 340 ET" means something only because blades are rare.

5 · Trading — the mechanics

The core transaction: a relic changes hands, ET moves the other way, atomically (never both-fail, never duplicate, never vanish).

Order book vs. direct swap

  • Order book (recommended): a seller lists "Musashi's Blade · 200 ET"; any buyer with 200 ET takes it. Prices emerge from supply and demand — and this is what feeds the marquee with real, moving numbers. Most "exchange-like."
  • Direct swap: A offers X for Y, B accepts. Simpler, but no price discovery, weaker marquee.

The atomic trade (the hard, must-be-perfect part)

Executing a trade must be a single, all-or-nothing operation: check buyer's ET ≥ price, transfer relic ownership, move ET (minus burned fee), record both in the ledger — or roll the whole thing back. This is the same care the minting logic already takes. A bug here duplicates or destroys player property; it is the crown-jewels code of the Exchange and belongs in a guarded, server-side RPC, never client trust.

6 · The marquee — finally honest, finally meaningful

The scrolling ticker (Musashi's sword, Moses' sandals) stops being decoration and becomes the live tape of the relic market: last trade prices, movements, volume — all real, all in ET. "MUSASHI'S BLADE ▲ 340 ET · MOSES' SANDALS ▼ 90 ET · 12 TRADES TODAY." It reads exactly like a trading terminal because it now is one — an in-game one, honest to the last number.

7 · The guardrails (non-negotiable)

  1. ET is never purchasable with real money. No "buy 1000 ET for $9.99." The instant this exists, ET is a financial instrument.
  2. ET is never cashable out. No withdrawing, no converting to money or crypto. Earned in, spent in, that is the whole loop.
  3. Relics have no stated real-world value. No "$" anywhere near them. Their only price is in ET.
  4. Server-authoritative. Balances, ownership, and trades live server-side (Supabase + a guarded worker). The client displays; it never decides.
  5. Watch for loot-box regulation. Prefer achievement-gated relic sources over pure paid randomness.
  6. The Glass Gate applies. The Exchange shows real market state or the empty box — never a stale or invented price.
These guardrails are not limitations — they are what let you build something rich and fun now, quickly, without a lawyer in the room. A thriving no-cash-value economy is also the best possible foundation if real value is ever added later: you would be adding value to a proven, living market, not building one from scratch under regulatory scrutiny.

8 · Build order (phased)

Phase 1 · The Exchange pane + honest marquee

Rename the terminal pane to The Exchange. Keep the live economy vitals (chart + ticker) we built. Add a marquee that shows real relic/economy activity. No trading yet — just the honest market view. (Ship this first; it is mostly done.)

Phase 2 · Relics exist

Relics table (id, name, figure, rarity, edition). Ownership ledger (who owns what). Earn relics through mastery. A "My Collection" view. Still no trading — just earning and owning. Establishes scarcity and the faucet.

Phase 3 · The shop (the great sink)

Spend ET to acquire relics from the source. This is the critical sink that gives ET value and fixes the "only goes up" problem. Burns ET on purchase.

Phase 4 · Player-to-player trading (the exchange proper)

Order book: list, buy, atomic swap, trade-fee burn. The marquee goes fully live with real trade prices. This is the meatiest, most careful build — server-authoritative, guarded RPC, all-or-nothing execution.

Phase 5 · Balance & tune

Watch faucet/sink ratio on the terminal. Adjust drop rates, fees, prices. An economy is never "done" — it is tended. The terminal is the instrument you tend it with.
