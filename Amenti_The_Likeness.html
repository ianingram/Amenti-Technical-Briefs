Four Owners · One Description · The Refusal of the Icon

STYLE holds that the description is the asset and the image is a cache. There is no description. Twenty-one hand-built SVGs are the only record of what any figure in this library looks like — which makes the cache the asset, and inverts the law that everything else rests on. This brief closes that gap, and closes a second one beside it: left to itself an engine draws the icon, and the icon is almost never the moment.

THE ICON IS THE STATISTICAL MODE OF THE TRAINING DATA.
THE PIVOTAL MOMENT IS ALMOST NEVER THE MODE.

1 · The evidence, computed from the live library

Age at the moment is not a stylistic preference. It is arithmetic on two numbers the fleet already holds — Birth-Date in the ledger, and the year in the quiz payload — and it is the single largest source of wrong images in the system today.

Keller was seven years old at the pump. Every image in the model's head is the adult. Draw the icon and you have not made a stylistic error — you have illustrated the wrong person, on the exact moment the quiz is about.

Columbus is the sharper case. He is not merely the wrong age in the icon, he is the wrong register: the quiz is about being arrested and shipped home in irons, and the icon is a conqueror on a prow. That render would contradict its own passage.

Why the basis is required and not optional

For most of a thousand figures, nobody knows what they looked like. Accuracy therefore cannot mean one thing, and the fleet already owns the right structure for this: life, canon and source, applied now to the face rather than the record.

unknown is a correct value, not a failure. “No reliable likeness survives” instructs the renderer to lean on dress and the signature object — and Hannibal is recognised by the elephant and the helmet, not by his cheekbones.

The cautionary case is already in the file. The Moses portrait carries <!-- HORN-LIGHT (the iconography from Michelangelo's Moses) -->. Those horns are a mistranslation — Hebrew keren, radiance, read as horns — that became eight centuries of art. Drawing them is RIGHT, because the subject is the tradition's Moses. Only a basis field lets the fleet say so honestly instead of asserting a likeness it does not have.

4 · The scene record

A scene is style-agnostic by construction: it says what is happening, never how it is drawn. One scene per quiz is nearly free — the engine has just researched that moment — plus whatever is written by hand. One per figure is marked primary: the card face.

Enums, not prose

set · time · place · shot · angle

Prose is where stereotype hides, because a model fills an unspecified field with its mode. An enumerated field cannot be filled with a mode; it can only be chosen, and a wrong choice is visible in a table.

The three planes

backgroundsilhouette only, flat, one or two tones. Pillars, treeline, crowd. Never detailed.
midgroundthe figure, fully rendered. Today this is the ONLY plane that exists.
foregroundone cropped, single-tone element. A spear butt, a table edge, a shoulder.

The foreground plane is the cheapest depth available in vector and the one currently missing entirely. It also gives the style validator something concrete to enforce: background may not use the skin gradient; foreground is single-tone.

5 · The refusals

The validator is not a review — it is a parser. Each rule below is machine-checkable, and a scene that fails one never reaches the renderer.

  1. A scene must name an age. Computed, not guessed: quiz year − Birth-Date. A scene with no age is refused. This one rule removes most of the icon problem.
  2. A scene must contain an action in progress and an object in a named state. “At a desk, thoughtful” is refused. “Tamping the pipe, the match flaring” passes. Churchill’s cigar has gone out; the tablets are mid-smash, not dropped.
  3. Prefer the adjacent moment. The Rubicon is not the crossing — it is the pause on the bank. The recantation is not the kneeling — it is standing up afterward. The instant before or after holds the decision rather than the outcome.
  4. No repeat within a figure’s stack. Before writing, read that figure’s existing scenes and forbid the same age band, shot, set and time. Four gates must yield four genuinely different images by rule. This is existingFor, pointed at scenes instead of moments.
  5. The accent belongs to the figure and survives the light. Light the SET; never repaint the figure. If twilight recolours everyone, every figure looks the same at twilight and the one colour that identifies them is gone.
  6. Honour the tradition’s own terms. Where a tradition forbids an image, draw the moment — the document, the city, the covenant — and leave the figure out. That is not a lesser portrait; it is the correct one.
  7. No caricature. Draw the person doing the thing they are known for, in the dress of their own culture as its own people would render it, at the age they were. The specificity that defeats cliché is the specificity that defeats caricature; they fail together and they are fixed together.
  8. A scene must teach. If the image only restates the caption it is decoration. Caesar working through two stacks of documents is the dictatorship in one gesture. A seven-year-old at a pump is the quiz.
SEEK UNIQUENESS IN THE CONTENT. SEEK COHESION IN THE STYLE.
VARY THE MOMENT, THE AGE, THE CAMERA, THE LIGHT.
NEVER VARY THE SPEC.

The twenty-one puppets read as one cast precisely because they share defs(), the 320×560 stage, and the pool of light. The moment one figure earns a bespoke treatment because they “deserve” it, the library becomes a folder of illustrations. That constraint is the asset.

6 · The reading room

A figure with four gates has four moments — a visual through-line of a life rather than one static portrait. The primary scene is the card face; the rest belong to the room. This also gives depth-2 and depth-3 quizzes something they presently lack: a reason to exist visually, and not merely as further questions.

Done when
Caesar holds two scenes that differ in age band, shot and set — and the validator refuses a third that repeats any of them.
Likeness IVMangasample three before committing

Manga is a VECTOR style — line art, flat tones, screentones, no gradients — which is what SVG does best and what the existing pipeline already produces. A new spec and a new validator; no provider, no bucket, no licensing question.

Sample Caesar, Rand and Gilgamesh: a fighter, a thinker, a giant. If the style cannot hold a woman at 14 combat holding a manuscript, it is not a style for this library.

Done when
Three figures render from description alone, recognisably the same people as their puppets.
Likeness VRetire, Never Deleteone word

Puppet becomes status='retired' in the styles table. The thirty-eight SVGs stay in the drawer. They are paid for, they work, and they are the fallback while manga sits below coverage.

Note
A style is offered at 100% coverage and at no other number. Manga at 40 of 999 does not qualify, and retiring puppet does not change that. Retirement is a display decision; coverage is a rendering programme.

8 · What must not be touched

Voice, Dialect and Gender are inside the audio cache key. composeStyle() folds dialect and voice into the /speak style string, and audioKey = sha256(TTS_MODEL + voice + STYLE + TEXT). Change one byte of a figure’s dialect and every clip ever rendered for them is orphaned: the archive keeps them, no invoice mentions the loss, and the calibration passage will report the drift.

ADDING A COLUMN IS FREE.
EDITING VOICE, DIALECT OR GENDER RE-BILLS THE ARCHIVE.
ENRICH BY ADDITION.

If the twenty-one-value voice vocabulary proves too coarse, do not rewrite it. Add VoiceNotes, and have composeStyle append it only for figures that carry one — so every unchanged figure keeps every cached second.

9 · Acceptance

the description testDelete a rendering and it regenerates recognisably. If it does not, the description is not the asset yet.
the age testAsk for keller-water and receive a seven-year-old.
the stack testA figure with four gates yields four images that differ in age, shot and set — by rule, not by luck.
the cast testThirty renders side by side read as one library, not thirty illustrations.
the honesty testEvery drawn face can name its basis, and the ones that cannot say unknown.
