TheCARD BAY

One Figure · One Card · Whatever the Depth

Gate rows were right when a figure held two quizzes and stop working at five. A card that grows with its stack breaks the grid it sits in, and the grid is what makes the arena scannable. So the stack moves off the card and into a bay behind it.

What rows do at seven gates, and what the bay does instead.

THE CARD STAYS A CARD.
ONE FIGURE, ONE TILE, UNIFORM HEIGHT, WHATEVER THE DEPTH.

The problem, stated exactly

A gate row is about forty pixels. Two rows sit comfortably beneath a portrait and stat bars. Seven rows is two hundred and eighty pixels of list on a tile that was two hundred tall to begin with — and the arena becomes a column of cards at wildly different heights, which is the one thing a grid cannot survive.

This is not hypothetical. Caesar already holds two and the engine is ready for depth 2 and 3 across the roster. The first time the patrol runs at depth, several figures reach five.

Click, not hover

Hover reveals fast and is hostile on touch, and this is a deliberate act rather than a preview. The card carries the count — “7 gates” — so a seeker knows a bay exists before committing to open it.

All at once, not paged

A seeker opening Jefferson wants to know what is there before spending ninety seconds. Four moments visible is orientation. Four moments one at a time is a corridor.

The strip is the argument

This is the part that matters beyond layout. Jefferson’s bay reads:

the Declaration  ·  Monticello  ·  the Louisiana Purchase  ·  Sally Hemings

That fourth title tells a seeker what kind of place this is before they answer a single question. A library that holds Jefferson and does not hold Hemings has made a choice; so has one that does. You cannot say that by paging — they would have to earn their way to it, and by then it is a reveal rather than a promise.

The same is true of Columbus: navigation and the Taíno, side by side, visible from the first look.

Locked cards show their titles

Dimmed, unclickable, and legible. Hiding a locked title would be the platform flinching at its own premise. Knowing that Jefferson’s fourth gate is Sally Hemings is exactly the thing worth earning your way to.

A PLACE BUILT TO WEIGH THE DEAD CANNOT BE COY ABOUT WHAT IT IS WEIGHING.

What this replaces, and what it inherits

Gate rows are superseded. They were correct for two and are the wrong shape for five. The bay takes their place.

The label work carries straight over. The short names written an hour before this decision — the Rubicon, the last words, the swan-neck flask — are exactly what a strip needs. That was built for rows and turns out to have been built for this.

And it gives the scenes somewhere to live. Each card in the bay carries its own moment’s picture, which is a thing a forty-pixel row was never going to do. Caesar at the river on one, the Senate floor on another.

Three decisions in one evening have now converged on the same dependency: the roster wants a style, the calendar dispatch wants the right picture of the right moment, and the bay wants a picture per card. All three are waiting on scenes. That is the strongest argument yet for building them next.
