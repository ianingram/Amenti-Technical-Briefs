Five Strata · One Floor · Two Directions

At the end of the ledger dedupe the captain asked whether the spreadsheet is the ground floor — nothing beneath it but the dead who are in the ground. It is, for one thing, and it is not, for another. That single distinction decides where every future repair belongs, and it is the whole of this document.

Why the floor is identity and not truth

A ledger row asserts a person. It does not assert a fact. THE QUIZZARD already says this outright, and it is the reason the engine was built the way it was:

“A library folder is NOT required. Lincoln has a full reading room; Leif has only this row. Both produce equally good quizzes, because the substance comes from verified research, not from pre-existing depth. The row is the anchor; history is the source.”

So the engine does not read history out of the spreadsheet. It reads a name, then goes past the floor with the search tool and verifies against the record itself. That is what the Links column is for, and it is why a model writing from memory is the single failure the whole doctrine exists to prevent.

Two loads, two paths. Identity rises from the floor and everything above inherits it. Verification ignores the floor entirely and goes to bedrock. Confuse them and you get a system that is confident about people who never existed.

AN ERROR AT THE FLOOR IS INVISIBLE FROM EVERY STOREY ABOVE IT.

The side door

One correction to the clean model, and it is factual rather than philosophical. The floor has a door in the side of it. Six curated figures were admitted straight into stratum III and appear in no ledger row at all:

musashi   hannibal   cleopatra
tacitus   manlius    frederick-douglass

This is not a defect. The curated set is a supplement by design, and mergeCuratedOver() exists precisely to layer it over the roll call. But it means the sentence “the ledger decides who exists” is true of the thousand and false of the thirty-eight — and any component that assumes a single point of admission will be wrong about eight people.

Rev. A said eight, and named Gibbon and Stoker among them. That count came from an older seven-column export of the ledger; both are present in the live twelve-column one. The lesson is the document’s own: a count taken from the wrong copy of the floor is wrong on every storey that quotes it.

Nothing was renamed, so art keys, topic ids, reading rooms, group maps, bookmarked URLs and every recorded seeker progress row kept working untouched. The full account is in VELVET RESOLVER.

What only the top floor does

Worth naming, because it is the reason the rest is built. Strata I through IV are retrieval and rendering — a dead person, recorded, dressed, and asked about. Nothing there creates anything that was not already in the world.

Stratum V does. When a seeker files a case to the docket and argues a legacy, a living human adds a thing that did not exist before — the only genuinely new matter in the whole section. Everything beneath it is scaffolding for that one act.

Which is also the correct priority ordering when time is short: a fault at the floor threatens the building; a fault at the top threatens the point of it.

The rules that fall out

  1. Repair at the stratum that owns the fault. If two rows describe one person, that is a ledger repair. Do not teach the matcher to cope.
  2. No storey may invent a person. A character, a quiz or a card may only name someone admitted at stratum II or through the side door. Anything else is a figure with no ground under it.
  3. Identity is assigned once, at the floor, and never re-derived. Six private derivations produced six answers. A key is stored, not computed — and an alias table is how the other five spellings are forgiven without being trusted.
  4. Verification skips the floor. The engine goes to bedrock with search. A row is an anchor and never a citation.
  5. Every stratum fails visibly. The glass gate applies at each level: show what was read or show nothing. A layer that quietly substitutes a plausible answer is worse than one that stops — and a fallback that swaps a thousand figures for thirty-eight without a word is only half a gate.
  6. Count people, not rows. The codex advertised 1,108 for months. There were about a thousand. A number nobody computes is decoration.
  7. Read the floor you are actually standing on. Rev. A miscounted the side door from a stale export. Every storey that quotes a floor count inherits its error.
