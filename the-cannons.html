TheCANNONS

Canon Mode · The Second Armament

The Quizzard asks whether a life can be verified. The Cannons ask what the sources actually say. One line changes — and the most argued texts in human memory come within range.

Why she needed a second gun

The Quizzard as built has one mode, and it verifies lives. That works down the historical spine — Caesar, Newton, Darwin, Lincoln — and it halts on a band of the roster that no one anticipated would matter so much.

Homer. Romulus. Theseus. Agamemnon. Prometheus. Gilgamesh. Isaac and Jacob. Hippolyta, queen of the Amazons. Ninety-nine mythic entries and the deep-antiquity names beside them — and they are the most recognised figures on the entire roster. The halts cluster precisely where public interest is highest.

Worse, these are the figures people already half-know, and half-wrongly. Ask who the famous Amazon queen was and a great many will answer Diana — which is the Roman goddess of the hunt, and also the name DC gave Wonder Woman, who is in the comics the daughter of Hippolyta. The confusion is entirely reasonable, and there is no correction layer anywhere. Nobody needs Lavoisier untangled. Everybody needs Hippolyta untangled.

THE HALT WAS NEVER "WE CANNOT ASK ABOUT HIPPOLYTA."
IT WAS ONLY EVER "WE CANNOT ASSERT THAT SHE LIVED."

The one line that changes

Everything in the Quizzard holds — six questions, the invariant, verified keys, the refusals. Only the target of verification moves.

Mode I — the original
LIFE
“Did this happen?”
Verified against the historical record: chronicles, archaeology, documents, scholarship. Produces sourceType: Event and Idea. Halts when no life can be confirmed.
Mode II — the second armament
CANON
“Does the tradition say this?”
Verified against the surviving texts: Apollodorus, Homer, the Hebrew Bible, Plutarch, the sagas. Produces sourceType: Canon. The claim is about the source, never about the past.

This is not a lowering of the bar. It is a different, equally hard question with an equally checkable answer. Apollodorus is as citable as Livy — he is simply being cited for what the Greeks told, not for what occurred. A key built on Library 2.5.9 cannot be wrong, because the line is right there.

The four rules that keep her honest

Rule 1 · The register

The passage is written in the voice of tradition and never asserts historicity. “As the sources tell it.” “In the myth.” “The text records.” A canon passage that slips into the historical register — “Hippolyta ruled the Amazons at Themiscyra” stated flatly — has broken the mode and must be rejected.

Rule 2 · Chapter and verse

Every key carries its citation: work, book, chapter, line. Not a bibliography for the trial — a source for the claim. Apollodorus, Library 2.5.9. 2 Samuel 11:15. This is the field the whole deep layer will one day hang from, and it cannot be retrofitted across a thousand trials.

Rule 3 · The conflict is the content

Where traditions disagree, the disagreement becomes the question rather than being flattened away. This is not a compromise; it is the best material in the mode, and it is where the hair-splitters live.

Was Theseus' Amazon bride Hippolyta or Antiope? The sources genuinely split — Apollodorus offers variants naming Melanippe or Antiope; Diodorus has Theseus taking Antiope as spoils on Heracles' expedition; Pherecydes and Hellanicus make his voyage a separate one entirely; Plutarch reports both an abduction by trickery and an elopement for love. A shallow trial flattens that to “the Amazon queen.” A deep one makes the split the question.

The technique is already proven: the leif-vinland trial wrote the sagas' disagreement into its own passage rather than hiding it.

Rule 4 · The duality is interpretive

The two cases argue how the tradition should be read, never whether it is true. For Hippolyta: an enduring image of female sovereignty and martial power, against a Greek male anxiety-fantasy in which women who refuse submission are, in every telling, defeated, abducted, or married off. Both are real scholarly positions. Neither requires anyone to believe or disbelieve anything.

The Sacred-Text Guardrail — binding

Canon mode ranges over scripture, and there the wrong frame does not produce a weak trial — it produces an insult. Three hard limits:

1. The trial examines the text and its interpretation. It never adjudicates faith, and it never asks a seeker to argue whether a religion is true.

2. The duality is drawn from arguments within the scholarly and religious traditions themselves — not from believer against unbeliever.

3. No generator improvises a duality for a religious figure. The frame for that class is written by the captain and supplied to the engine. This is the single place in the whole fleet where autonomy is withheld by design.

Reference implementation · david-uriah

The story is well known across the English-speaking world and halts instantly in LIFE mode: no independent attestation exists for David's affair or Uriah's killing. In CANON mode it is one of the strongest trials on the roster, because the moral argument is already inside the source — the text does not excuse the king; it convicts him, and makes him convict himself.
